# Family Decision & Approval Workflow — Master Index

> Research on multi-person trip decision-making, family approval workflows, itinerary voting, disagreement resolution, co-planning tools, and group consensus building for the Waypoint OS platform.

---

## Series Overview

This series covers the family decision workflow — the invisible negotiation that happens before any booking is made. Indian travel decisions are rarely individual: spouses debate budgets, grandparents veto adventure activities, teenagers want nightlife while parents want culture, and the family friend who "traveled there last year" influences the destination. From shared itinerary voting and approval chains (spouse approves → parents review → finalize) to disagreement resolution (compromise itineraries, activity splits), family decision-making is the most underserved part of the travel booking journey.

**Target Audience:** Product managers, UX designers, agents

**Key Insight:** The average Indian family trip involves 3-5 decision-makers and takes 2-4 weeks of deliberation before booking. During this time, 30% of families switch to a different agency because the original agency couldn't facilitate the family's internal decision process. The agency that provides a "family co-planning" tool — where everyone votes, comments, and reaches consensus in one place — captures the 70% of bookings that currently go to the fastest proposer, not the best one.

## Key Themes

### 1. Indian Travel Decisions Are Group Decisions
A spouse who wasn't consulted is a spouse who vetoes. A grandparent who wasn't told about the trekking day will refuse to go. Every trip must be designed for the entire group, and every group member must feel heard before booking.

### 2. The Fastest Proposal Doesn't Always Win
Speed matters (first proposal wins 60%), but a proposal that the whole family has already approved internally wins 90%. Facilitating the internal family alignment before the proposal gives the agency an unassailable advantage.

### 3. Disagreement Is Data
When the husband votes for Bali and the wife votes for Thailand, the agent learns that budget vs. experience is the tension. A "compromise itinerary" that addresses both preferences wins the booking and earns trust.

### 4. WhatsApp Groups Are the Default Planning Tool
Indian families don't use planning apps — they use WhatsApp groups. The agency must integrate INTO the family's WhatsApp group, not ask the family to adopt a new tool.
