# Simulated User Interview: Family Coordinator (End Traveler)

**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Format:** Simulated depth interview (role-played)
**Duration:** ~50 minutes (simulated)
**Interviewer:** Product Researcher
**Participant:** "Meera" — Family Coordinator / Frequent Traveler (Persona S2)

---

## 1. Participant Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Name** | Meera Iyer (pseudonym) |
| **Age** | 38 |
| **Location** | Bangalore, India |
| **Role** | Family trip coordinator for 3 families (15 people total) |
| **Travel Frequency** | 2-3 big trips/year + 4-5 weekend getaways |
| **Last 3 Trips** | Singapore (2023), Thailand (2024), Europe (2025) |
| **Preferred Booking** | Through trusted agent (Priya) — never direct OTA |
| **Tech Comfort** | Moderate — uses WhatsApp, Google Maps, basic apps |

## 2. Interview Scenario**

**Context:** Meera coordinated a 15-person trip to Singapore via Priya (who uses Agency OS). She's experienced the "before" (Thailand 2024 — manual agent) and "after" (Singapore 2025 — agent using the tool). This interview explores the client-side experience.

---

## 3. Interview Transcript**

### Opening: The Coordinator Reality**

**Interviewer:** Meera, what does it mean to be the "family coordinator"?**

**Meera:** *(laughs)* It means I'm the unofficial travel agent for 15 people who don't agree on anything. 

Let me paint the picture. For the Thailand trip last year, I had 3 WhatsApp groups going. One for my family (4 people), one for my brother's family (5 people), one for my parents-in-law. Everyone had opinions:

- My 6-year-old wanted "water slides"
- My 10-year-old wanted "science museums"  
- My husband wanted "beer and beach"
- My father-in-law wanted "no walking, air-conditioned places"
- My mother-in-law wanted "vegetarian food everywhere"
- My brother wanted "under ₹80K per person"

I had to collect ALL of this and send it to the agent. And then when the agent sent options, I had to forward them to 3 groups, collect feedback from 15 people, resolve conflicts ("No, we can't do a 6 AM tour with grandma"), and send a consolidated response back.

It's like being a diplomat. Between 3 families AND the agent.

**Interviewer:** And how did the Thailand trip go?**

**Meera:** The agent did her best. But... the options she sent were generic. "Standard Singapore/Thailand package." It didn't account for the fact that my father-in-law can't walk more than 200 meters without resting. Or that my 6-year-old gets overstimulated in crowded places.

We ended up changing plans 3 times DURING the trip. "This hotel is too far from the beach for grandma." "This activity is too crowded for the kid." Each change cost us money and stress.

---

### Section 1: The Singapore Experience (With Agency OS)**

**Interviewer:** Now tell me about Singapore. What was different?**

**Meera:** Oh, everything. 

First, when I sent the inquiry to Priya, she responded the SAME DAY. Not 3 days later. She sent me a message that basically said: "Got it, Meera. Quick questions to make sure I get this right for 15 people..."

And the questions were... specific. Not generic "any preferences?" but:

- "How much walking is comfortable for the elderly members?"
- "Are the kids okay with crowds or should we avoid peak hours?"
- "Strict vegetarian for everyone or just some members?"
- "Is ₹1.2L per person firm or can we stretch for better hotels?"

I remember thinking: "She actually read what I sent before."

Then — and this was the big one — when she sent the options, each one had a section called "Why this works for YOUR group." Not "Why this is a great hotel." But "Why this works for 15 people including elderly and kids."

For example: "Hotel X — has elevators (no stairs for in-laws), kids' pool (keeps the 6-year-old busy), vegetarian restaurant in the same building, 10-minute transfer to the park (grandma doesn't need to walk)."

I read that and thought: "She GETS it." This is the first time an agent acknowledged that we're not just 15 tourists. We're 15 people with very different needs.

**Interviewer:** How did the family respond?**

**Meera:** They loved it. For the first time, I didn't have to explain the options to everyone. The "Why this works for YOUR group" section was so clear that even my father-in-law understood why Hotel X was chosen.

Usually, I have to translate: "This hotel is good BECAUSE..." Now, Priya's options did the explaining for me.

We had ONE round of revisions. Just one. For Thailand, we had THREE. And even after that, we were changing things during the trip.

---

### Section 2: The Revision Experience**

**Interviewer:** You mentioned one round of revisions. What happened?**

**Meera:** My brother looked at Option 1 and said: "Can we add Sentosa? The kids really want the cable car."

In the old days, I'd message the agent, she'd redo the WHOLE thing, send a new PDF 2 days later. 

This time, Priya responded in 4 hours with an updated option: "Added Sentosa day with cable car. Your budget goes up by ₹15K per family, but here's how to adjust: shift one night from Hotel X to Hotel Y (still suitable for elderly)."

She showed the TRADE-OFF. That's the key. She didn't just say "yes, added." She said "yes, but here's what changes."

For a coordinator managing 3 families, that trade-off visibility is EVERYTHING. I can go back to my brother and say: "Yes, we can add Sentosa, but we need to downgrade one hotel night. Is that okay?"

He said yes. Done. No back-and-forth.

**Interviewer:** What if the tool hadn't shown the trade-off?**

**Meera:** Then I'd get the new quote, see the price jump, and either (a) panic and say no, or (b) say yes without understanding why it's more expensive.

The trade-off section made me feel like Priya was being transparent with me. Not hiding costs. Not surprising me later.

---

### Section 3: During the Trip**

**Interviewer:** How was the actual trip experience?**

**Meera:** Smooth. For the first time, I wasn't the "problem solver" during the trip.

Usually, something goes wrong: hotel doesn't have the extra bed, transport is late, someone's dietary restriction gets ignored. And everyone looks at ME because I'm the coordinator.

This time, Priya had sent a "Trip Master Record" — basically a one-page summary with:

- All confirmation numbers
- Emergency contacts (Priya's number, hotel numbers, transport numbers)
- Daily schedule with pickup times
- Special notes: "Father-in-law needs elevator access" / "Kids' meals: no spice"
- Document checklist: "All passports submitted? Yes, 15/15 ✓"

When my brother's flight was delayed by 3 hours, I just checked the master record, saw the transport contact, called them, and they adjusted the pickup time. No drama.

**Interviewer:** And post-trip?**

**Meera:** This is where it still breaks down. 

After we returned, Priya sent a "Hope you had a great trip!" message. I responded with photos and a "thank you." And then... nothing.

Six months later, I'm planning our next trip (Japan, 15 people again). I messaged Priya. She responded warmly: "Meera! Great to hear from you. Let me pull up your Singapore details..."

And then she sent me generic options again. She didn't remember that my father-in-law now uses a walker. She didn't remember that we loved Hotel X's vegetarian restaurant. She didn't remember that the 6-year-old's favorite was the water park.

It's like we were STRANGERS again. All that experience from Singapore? Gone.

---

### Section 4: The Ideal Experience**

**Interviewer:** If you could design the perfect client experience, what would it look like?**

**Meera:** *(thinks for a long time)*

Okay, imagine this:

1. **A shared portal.** Not WhatsApp groups. A proper page where ALL 15 of us can see the trip options, comment on them, vote on preferences. My father-in-law doesn't want to be on WhatsApp at 9 PM debating hotel choices. He wants to look at options on a Sunday afternoon with his chai.

2. **Preference collection per person.** Not "what does the group want?" but "Meera wants culture, kids want water slides, dad wants AC." Everyone fills out a 5-minute form. The agent gets a COMPREHENSIVE brief.

3. **Live status tracking.** "Where is our planning at? Documents collected: 12/15. Options generation: in progress. Booking: pending." Right now, I'm chasing Priya: "Where are we with the visa?" With a portal, I'd SEE it.

4. **Post-trip memory.** After Singapore, the system should remember: "Meera's group loves vegetarian food, prefers hotels with elevators, kids enjoyed water activities." When I message for Japan, Priya should ALREADY know this.

5. **Change requests without starting over.** If we want to shift from 3 days Singapore to 4 days, the system should show: "Here's what changes in your itinerary, budget, and feasibility." Not a whole new proposal.

**Interviewer:** That's a long wish list. Which one matters most?**

**Meera:** The memory one. Definitely. 

I'm a loyal customer. I've given Priya ₹15L+ in bookings. But if she can't remember that my father-in-law now uses a walker, or that we hated that one hotel in Bangkok... it feels like she doesn't CARE. 

OTAs don't remember either. But I pay Priya a commission because she's SUPPOSED to be personal. If she can't remember me after 3 trips, why am I not just booking on MakeMyTrip?

---

### Section 5: Trust and AI**

**Interviewer:** How would you feel if you knew an AI helped plan your trip?**

**Meera:** *(hesitates)* Hmm. 

If it's helping Priya do her job better — asking better questions, remembering things — I'm fine with it. Actually, I'd be happy. It means she's using modern tools to serve me better.

But if I felt like NO ONE actually looked at my trip... like, if I got a generic AI-generated proposal that didn't account for my father-in-law's walker? Then I'd be upset. 

It's like... I want Priya to use AI as a TOOL, not hide behind it. When she sent those "Why this works for YOUR group" notes, I could tell SHE wrote them. Or at least, she reviewed them. That personal touch matters.

If I ever felt like a robot planned my vacation, I'd switch agents. Immediately.

---

### Section 6: Pricing Sensitivity**

**Interviewer:** Would you pay extra for a "premium" client experience?**

**Meera:** No. *(firm)*

I'm already paying Priya a commission. If she uses a tool that makes her more efficient, that's HER business advantage, not mine. 

What I WILL do is: if her service is noticeably better because of the tool, I'll refer friends. And I'll keep coming back. That's the "payment" — loyalty and referrals.

But if she tried to charge me extra for "AI-enhanced planning"? I'd laugh. I'd say: "Priya, I'm already paying you ₹50K commission on this trip. The tool is YOUR cost of doing business, not mine."

---

### Section 7: Dream Feature**

**Interviewer:** Your magic wand?**

**Meera:** A "Family Portal" that I can share with all 15 people.

Not a WhatsApp group. A proper page where:
- Everyone sees the same options (no "I didn't get the email" excuses)
- Everyone can comment/vote on preferences
- Priya posts updates and everyone sees them
- Document collection: each person uploads their own passport
- Post-trip: everyone rates the hotels/activities, and it builds a family travel profile

Right now, I'm the human WhatsApp relay. The portal would take me OUT of the middle.

And the best part? When we book Japan next year, Priya opens our family profile and says: "Last time, you rated the vegetarian restaurant 5/5. I've included similar options in Tokyo."

That's when I'd say: "Priya, you're not just an agent. You're family."

## 4. Key Findings**

### Finding 1: The Coordinator Is an Overloaded Middleman**

Meera manages 3 WhatsApp groups + the agent. She's translating agent outputs to 15 people and consolidating 15 opinions back to the agent. The tool helps the agent, but doesn't help Meera in her coordinator role.

**Implication:** A "Group Coordinator Portal" — shared view for multi-family trips — would eliminate the WhatsApp relay and reduce Meera's coordination burden. High value for group bookings (the highest-margin segment).

### Finding 2: "Why This Works for YOU" Is the Magic Phrase**

The single most impactful element of Priya's new output was contextualizing each recommendation: "Hotel X works for YOUR group because..." This made Meera feel understood and eliminated her need to explain options to family members.

**Implication:** Every client-facing output MUST include a "Why this fits YOUR specific needs" section. This is the differentiator vs. OTA generic packages. Not optional.

### Finding 3: Trade-Off Transparency Builds Trust**

When Priya added Sentosa, she showed the trade-off (hotel downgrade to offset cost). Meera felt she was being treated transparently, not "sold to." This built trust vs. confusion/anxiety.

**Implication:** Every revision/change must show: "Here's what changes in budget, itinerary, and feasibility." Never surprise the client with a price jump without explanation.

### Finding 4: Post-Trip Memory Is the Loyalty Driver**

The biggest gap: Priya didn't remember Singapore preferences 6 months later. For a loyal customer who's spent ₹15L+, this feels like being treated as a stranger. The OTA doesn't remember either — but Priya charges a premium specifically to be BETTER than OTAs.

**Implication:** Traveler memory/profile must persist across trips. At minimum: "Last trip: liked X, disliked Y, special needs: Z." This is the moat vs. OTAs.

### Finding 5: Clients Don't Want to Pay for the Tool**

Meera is clear: the commission she pays Priya is for Priya's service. If Priya uses tools to improve that service, great — but Meera won't pay extra. The value to Priya is retention + referrals, not direct client revenue.

**Implication:** Don't pitch "premium AI planning" as a client upcharge. Instead, use it to increase agent retention and referrals. The ROI is in LTV, not one-time fees.

### Finding 6: AI Is Acceptable Only as a Behind-the-Scenes Tool**

Meera is comfortable with AI helping Priya — as long as the outputs feel personal and Priya clearly reviewed them. If she ever feels "a robot planned my vacation," she'd switch agents.

**Implication:** All client-facing outputs must feel authored by the agent. No "Generated by AI" disclaimers. The tool is invisible; the agent's personality is visible.

### Finding 7: During-Trip Support Is Underserved**

The "Trip Master Record" was the most valuable during-trip artifact. But it's a static document. Meera still had to call Priya for the flight delay. A live "during-trip" view (pickup status, daily schedule, emergency contacts) would reduce her stress significantly.

**Implication:** Build a "Trip Active" view that the coordinator can check anytime during travel. Pickup status, today's schedule, emergency contacts, and a "HELP" button that alerts the agent.

### Finding 8: Group Coordination Is a Separate Product Surface**

WhatsApp groups are Meera's reality, but they're terrible for structured decision-making. A shared portal where 15 people can see options, comment, and vote would transform the group booking experience.

**Implication:** Even a lightweight "share link" that shows trip options + comment/voting would differentiate from OTAs. Full portal (per-person preference collection, document upload) is the v2.

## 5. Prioritized Recommendations (Client Experience Perspective)**

| Priority | Feature | Why | Client Impact |
|----------|---------|-----|---------------|
| **P0** | "Why this fits YOU" in all outputs | The single biggest trust-builder | Feels heard, not sold to |
| **P0** | Traveler memory/profile across trips | Prevents "stranger" feeling on repeat bookings | Loyalty and LTV |
| **P1** | Trade-off visibility on all changes | Transparency = trust | No surprise price jumps |
| **P1** | Trip Master Record (during-trip view) | Reduces coordinator stress | Smoother travel experience |
| **P2** | Shareable trip link for families | Eliminates WhatsApp relay | Less coordinator burden |
| **P2** | Per-person preference collection | Comprehensive brief, no assumptions | Better first-draft accuracy |
| **P3** | Group voting/commenting on options | Democratic family decision-making | Less conflict, faster consensus |
| **P3** | Post-trip feedback collection | Builds the memory corpus | Better future trips |
