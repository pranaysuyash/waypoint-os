# Simulated User Interview: Solo Travel Agent

**Date:** 2026-04-27
**Format:** Simulated depth interview (role-played)
**Duration:** ~50 minutes (simulated)
**Interviewer:** Product Researcher
**Participant:** "Priya" — Solo Travel Agent (Persona P1)

---

## 1. Participant Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Name** | Priya Sharma (pseudonym) |
| **Age** | 34 |
| **Location** | Mumbai, India |
| **Role** | Solo Travel Agent / Owner, "Priya Travels" |
| **Experience** | 7 years in travel planning |
| **Team Size** | 1 (herself) + 1 part-time assistant for paperwork |
| **Active Clients** | ~30-40 per year |
| **Specialization** | Family trips, honeymoons, Southeast Asia and Europe |
| **Tech Stack** | WhatsApp, Google Sheets, Excel, phone calls, Instagram DMs |
| **Monthly Revenue** | ₹2.5-4L (commission-based) |

## 2. Interview Scenario

**Context:** Priya was invited to test an early prototype of the "Agency OS" product. She has used it for 3 simulated trips over 2 weeks. The interview explores her real daily workflow, pain points, and reaction to the tool.

---

## 3. Interview Transcript (Edited for Clarity)

### Opening: Establishing Context

**Interviewer:** Thanks for making time, Priya. Before we talk about the tool, tell me about a typical workday.

**Priya:** Typical? There isn't one. But let me think... Okay, yesterday. I woke up at 7, checked WhatsApp — 23 unread messages across 8 conversations. Three were new inquiries from the weekend. One was a client who's in Bali right now, her hotel didn't have the extra bed she requested. Another was a family asking if I can move their Thailand trip from June to July because their kid's exams got extended.

By 10 AM I'd sent maybe 15 messages, made 3 phone calls to hotels in Bali, and I still hadn't had breakfast. The Bali thing took 2 hours to resolve because the hotel front desk kept saying "we'll check" and the client was panicking.

**Interviewer:** How do you keep track of all these conversations?

**Priya:** *(laughs)* That's the thing. I don't. I mean, I try. I have a Google Sheet with every client — their trip status, where we are in the process, pending items. But the sheet is always out of date. I update it when I remember, which is... not often enough. Last week I accidentally sent the same proposal to two different clients because I got confused about which WhatsApp thread was which.

**Interviewer:** What happened?

**Priya:** Mortifying. One was a ₹2L budget and the other was ₹8L. The budget client got a proposal way out of their range and thought I was trying to upsell them. I had to call and explain. They stayed, but it was embarrassing.

---

### Section 1: The Lead Intake Pain

**Interviewer:** Walk me through what happens when a new inquiry comes in.

**Priya:** Someone sends me a WhatsApp message. Usually it's something vague like "Hey Priya, we want to go somewhere nice in May. Family of 5. Budget maybe 3-4 lakhs?" And that's it. That's the whole message.

From there, I need to figure out:
- Where do they actually want to go? "Somewhere nice" could be anything.
- Who are these 5 people? Kids? Elderly?
- What does "3-4 lakhs" include? Just flights and hotel or everything?
- When in May? Early? Late? Are dates flexible?
- Have they been anywhere before? Did they like it?

So I send them a list of questions. And then I wait. Sometimes they reply in an hour, sometimes in 3 days. And when they reply, it's partial. They'll answer 3 out of 7 questions. So I ask the remaining ones. And we go back and forth.

**Interviewer:** How long does this intake process typically take?

**Priya:** From first message to having enough information to start researching? Three to five days. Sometimes longer if they're not responsive. And during that time, I have 8 other conversations going on simultaneously. So I'm constantly context-switching.

The worst part is — I know I'm not asking the right questions sometimes. I've been doing this for 7 years and I still forget to ask about things like: does anyone have knee problems? Is the elderly mother coming or is she just thinking about it? Are the kids in school during those dates? These things matter SO much for the itinerary, but they don't come up in the first round of questions.

---

### Section 2: The Research & Proposal Pain

**Interviewer:** Once you have the information, what happens next?

**Priya:** Research. This is the most time-consuming part. Let me give you a real example from last month. Family of 4, France and Italy, 10 days, ₹4.5L budget. I spent:
- 45 minutes on Skyscanner and airline sites checking flight prices and routes
- 1 hour on hotel options across Paris, Nice, Rome, and Florence
- 30 minutes cross-referencing with my preferred hotels (I have relationships with some properties)
- 20 minutes checking visa requirements and processing times
- 30 minutes putting together a day-by-day itinerary draft
- 45 minutes creating a formatted proposal in Canva so it looks professional

Total: about 4 hours. And this is a trip I've done variations of many times. For a new destination, it's 6-8 hours.

**Interviewer:** Do you reuse previous proposals?

**Priya:** I try. But every client is different. The Mehta family's France trip can't just be copied for the Guptas because the Guptas have a toddler and the Mehtas have teenagers. So I end up modifying everything anyway. The bones are similar, but the details change every time.

**Interviewer:** What happens after you send the proposal?

**Priya:** The revision loop. *(sighs)* This is where it gets painful. They'll say things like:
- "Can we swap the Amalfi Coast for something else? We heard it's too touristy."
- "My brother-in-law might join. Can we add one more person?"
- "Actually, can we do Greece instead of Italy?"
- "We found a cheaper flight on IndiGo. Can you match that timing?"

Each revision means going back to the drawing board. And because everything is interconnected — change the flight dates and the hotel availability changes, change the destination and the visa requirements change, add a person and the budget math changes — a "small change" is never actually small.

**Interviewer:** How many revisions does a typical trip go through?

**Priya:** Average? Two and a half. But I had one client who went through 7 revisions before booking. Seven. And then they cancelled two weeks before departure. That was a ₹40,000 loss because I'd already paid deposits on their behalf.

---

### Section 3: In-Trip and Post-Trip

**Interviewer:** What about after the trip is booked?

**Priya:** That's actually when the real stress starts. Before the trip, if something goes wrong, I have time to fix it. During the trip, it's crisis mode. My phone is on 24/7 when clients are traveling. Last month I got a call at 2 AM because a family in Phuket couldn't find their airport transfer. I had to call the Thai driver's number, which was in my phone from a booking confirmation 3 weeks earlier. Thank God I save everything.

**Interviewer:** And after they return?

**Priya:** Honestly? Nothing. I send a "hope you had a great trip!" message. Sometimes they reply with photos. Sometimes they don't. Then 6 months later they might message me again for their next trip, and I'm scrambling to remember: did they like that hotel? Were the kids comfortable? Did they mention wanting to try Japan next?

I keep notes, but they're scattered. Some in WhatsApp chat history, some in my Google Sheet, some in my head. When repeat clients come back, I feel like I'm starting from scratch with them. That's a terrible feeling. These people trust me with their vacations, and I can't even remember that Mrs. Kapoor hates early morning flights.

---

### Section 4: Reaction to the Agency OS Prototype

**Interviewer:** Now let's talk about the tool you've been testing. First impressions?

**Priya:** I was skeptical. I've tried travel CRMs before — they're all built for big agencies with 20 agents. Too complex, too expensive, and they don't understand how I actually work. I work on WhatsApp. I work on instinct. I don't have time to fill in 15 form fields for every lead.

But when I pasted that first message — the vague "we want to go somewhere nice" one — and it came back with the extracted intent AND follow-up questions... that got my attention. Because those were good questions. Questions I would ask, but maybe not all of them in the first round.

**Interviewer:** What specifically impressed you?

**Priya:** Two things. One, it caught the ambiguity I would have missed in a hurry. Like, it flagged "May" as unspecific and asked about school schedules. I would have just assumed mid-May. Two, the budget feasibility warning. I had a client who wanted Maldives for a family of 6 at ₹3L. I knew that was impossible but I didn't want to say no immediately. The tool basically said "this is not feasible — here's what's realistic." That saves me from an awkward conversation AND from wasting time researching impossible options.

**Interviewer:** What didn't work well?

**Priya:** A lot, actually. Let me be specific:

1. **I can't generate a proposal.** The tool understands the client's request beautifully. But then... I still have to go build the proposal myself in Canva. The research part is what takes the most time, and the tool doesn't do that part yet.

2. **No client history.** I pasted a message from a repeat client, and the tool treated him like a new lead. I've planned 3 trips for the Malhotras. The system should know that.

3. **No WhatsApp integration.** I'm copying and pasting back and forth. In reality, I need the tool to sit between me and WhatsApp. Maybe as an overlay or a bot that I can CC.

4. **No trip workspace.** Once I have 10 trips going, I need to click into each one and see: where are we? What's pending? What's the client waiting on from me? Right now it's one-at-a-time, which doesn't match how I work.

5. **No follow-up reminders.** I sent a proposal to a client and forgot about them for a week. The system should nudge me: "Hey, you sent a proposal to the Sharmas 5 days ago and they haven't responded. Want to follow up?"

**Interviewer:** Those are substantial gaps. If we could only fix two of those in the next month, which two?

**Priya:** *(without hesitating)* Proposal generation and trip workspace. If I can generate a decent first-draft proposal in 2 minutes instead of 4 hours, that changes everything. And if I can see all my trips in one place with their status, I stop dropping balls.

The WhatsApp thing is important but I can live with copy-paste for now. The history thing is important but it builds over time — the sooner I start, the sooner it accumulates.

---

### Section 5: Pricing and Willingness to Pay

**Interviewer:** If this tool worked the way you just described — proposals, workspace, follow-ups — what would you pay?

**Priya:** Right now, I spend about 40-45 hours a week on client work. If this tool cuts that to 25-30 hours — which it could if the proposal generation is good — that's 15 extra hours. 15 hours I can use to take on 5-8 more clients per month. At an average commission of ₹15,000 per trip, that's ₹75,000 to ₹1.2L more per month.

So... ₹3,000 to ₹5,000 per month feels right. Maybe more if it actually delivers on the proposal part. I'd pay ₹8,000 if it generates proposals that I only need to tweak, not build from scratch.

**Interviewer:** Would you pay per-trip instead?

**Priya:** No. I hate variable costs. I want to know what I'm paying every month so I can plan. If I have a slow month, I don't want to pay less and then get surprised when business picks up. Flat monthly fee. Predictable.

**Interviewer:** Would a free version with limits work for you?

**Priya:** Maybe to start. Like, 3 trips per month free, then pay. That would let me test it properly. But honestly, if it works, I'd just pay. I spend more than that on coffee every month.

---

### Section 6: Adoption Concerns

**Interviewer:** Would other agents you know use this?

**Priya:** Depends on the agent. The older agents — no. They plan trips in their head and they're proud of it. My colleague Sunita has been doing this for 15 years with a paper diary. She won't change.

But younger agents? Solo agents like me who are drowning? Absolutely. There's a WhatsApp group I'm in with about 40 independent travel agents. Every day someone posts: "I'm so overwhelmed" or "I missed a client's birthday follow-up" or "I spent 5 hours on a proposal and they booked with MakeMyTrip instead." This tool solves the exact problem we all complain about.

**Interviewer:** What would make you stop using it?

**Priya:** Three things:
1. If it gives wrong information. Like, if it says a visa isn't required when it is, or recommends a hotel that's permanently closed. Trust is everything in my business. One bad recommendation can lose a client forever.
2. If it's slow. I'm already impatient. If I paste a message and wait more than 30 seconds, I'll tab away and forget about it.
3. If my clients find out an AI is planning their trip. They're paying for MY expertise. The tool is my assistant, not my replacement. It needs to be invisible to the end client.

---

### Section 7: Dream Feature

**Interviewer:** Last question. If you could wave a magic wand and get one feature that doesn't exist today, what would it be?

**Priya:** *(thinks for a long time)*

A client memory that actually works. Not just trip history. I want the system to remember that Mr. Joshi always tips 20% so don't worry about the budget there. That Mrs. Iyer gets motion sickness on ferries so never suggest island-hopping by boat. That the Reddy family's daughter is obsessed with pandas so if there's a zoo with pandas near their destination, flag it.

These are the things that make my clients say "Priya knows us so well." It's not about the hotel or the flight. It's about the personal touches that make them come back to me instead of booking on MakeMyTrip.

If the system could absorb that — from my WhatsApp conversations, from my notes, from past trip feedback — and surface it at the right time? That would be worth more than any proposal generator. Because proposals are a commodity. Personal relationships are not.

## 4. Key Findings

### Finding 1: The "Last Mile" Problem Is the Biggest Pain

The intake and understanding phase (which the current product handles well) is only 15-20% of Priya's work. The remaining 80-85% — research, proposal creation, revision management, booking coordination, in-trip support — is where she spends the bulk of her time and where she feels the most pain.

**Implication:** The product's strongest feature (intent extraction + question generation) solves a real problem but an undersized one. The next priority should be proposal/option generation, which compresses the single most time-consuming activity.

### Finding 2: Multi-Trip Context Is Non-Negotiable

Priya never works on one trip at a time. She has 8-15 active conversations simultaneously. A tool that processes one inquiry at a time without workspace/scoping is like giving a chef one burner when they need six.

**Implication:** Trip-scoped workspace (with status tracking, pending actions, and follow-up reminders) is a prerequisite for adoption, not a nice-to-have.

### Finding 3: Repeat Client Recognition Is a Trust Signal

Priya's competitive advantage over OTAs (MakeMyTrip, Booking.com) is personal knowledge of her clients. When the system treats repeat clients as strangers, it undermines her professional identity. She needs the system to enhance her memory, not replace it.

**Implication:** Customer identity + trip history lookup should be treated as a trust-layer feature, not just a data feature. It needs to be visible and surfaced at intake time.

### Finding 4: Invisible AI Is the Only Acceptable AI

Priya is enthusiastic about AI assistance but adamant that her clients should never perceive AI involvement. The tool must be framed and function as a "copilot" — enhancing her work, not replacing her judgment. Client-facing outputs must feel authored by Priya.

**Implication:** All client-facing outputs (proposals, follow-up messages, itineraries) need agent personalization — their name, their style, their voice. No "Generated by AI" disclaimers.

### Finding 5: WhatsApp Is the Operating System

Priya doesn't work in a browser or an app. She works in WhatsApp. Any tool that requires her to leave WhatsApp to do her core work adds friction, regardless of how powerful it is. Copy-paste is acceptable short-term but will become a dealbreaker as volume increases.

**Implication:** WhatsApp integration (either via API, WhatsApp Business, or a browser extension overlay) is a medium-term necessity. Short-term: optimize the copy-paste flow to be as fast as possible.

### Finding 6: Pricing Tolerance Is Higher Than Expected

Priya's stated willingness to pay (₹3,000-8,000/month) is significant for a solo agent earning ₹2.5-4L/month. The key insight: she calculates value in terms of time recovered and additional clients she can serve, not in abstract "efficiency."

**Implication:** Pricing communication should be framed as "how many more clients can you serve?" not "how much time will you save?" The commercial ROI is the story.

### Finding 7: Trust Asymmetry — Wrong Information Is Existential

A bad hotel recommendation, a missed visa requirement, or an incorrect budget estimate doesn't just cause inconvenience — it destroys Priya's professional credibility. The tolerance for factual errors in travel planning is near zero.

**Implication:** The system needs confidence indicators and human-in-the-loop verification for any factual claim (visa requirements, hotel status, pricing). Never present uncertain information as certain.

### Finding 8: The "Client Memory" Is the Dream Feature

The single most desired capability — deep, accumulated knowledge of client preferences, quirks, and past experiences — doesn't exist in any tool Priya has tried. This is the moat. If the system can build a rich traveler profile over time that surfaces contextually ("Mrs. Iyer gets motion sickness — avoid ferry for this island itinerary"), it becomes indispensable.

**Implication:** Traveler profile/memory should be architected early, even if the initial implementation is simple (key-value notes per client). The data accumulation starts on day one.

## 5. Prioritized Recommendations

Based on the interview findings:

| Priority | Feature | Why | Effort |
|----------|---------|-----|--------|
| **P0** | Proposal/Option Generator | Solves biggest time sink (4 hours → minutes) | High |
| **P0** | Trip Workspace with Status Tracking | Multi-trip context is prerequisite for adoption | Medium |
| **P1** | Follow-Up Reminders & Ghost Detection | Prevents dropped leads, matches existing lifecycle spec | Low-Medium |
| **P1** | Client Identity + History Lookup | Trust signal for repeat clients, competitive moat | Medium |
| **P2** | WhatsApp Integration (Basic) | Reduces friction in primary workflow | Medium-High |
| **P2** | Traveler Memory/Notes | Builds long-term moat, highest dream-feature demand | Medium |
| **P3** | Confidence Indicators on Factual Claims | Prevents trust-destroying errors | Low |
