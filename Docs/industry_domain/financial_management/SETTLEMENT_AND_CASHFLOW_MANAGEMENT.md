# Settlement and Cashflow Management for Travel Agencies

## Overview
This document explains how travel agencies manage vendor settlement cycles, client payments, and cashflow timing.

## Core settlement concepts
- **Accounts receivable**: client invoices, retainers, and prepayments due before travel
- **Accounts payable**: supplier invoices, commissions owed, and markups payable after settlement
- **Cashflow gap**: the period between collecting client funds and paying suppliers

## Common settlement models
### Net settlement
- The agency remits the supplier net of commissions and fees.
- Useful when the agency issues tickets on its own IATA or BSP account.

### Gross settlement
- The agency pays the full supplier value and retains commissions separately.
- Common when the agent needs to show transparent pricing to corporate clients.

### Hybrid settlement
- The agency combines net and gross terms depending on product type, geography, and supplier credit.
- Example: airline tickets might be net, while hotel rooms are gross.

## Cashflow controls
- **Advance collection**: collect the full trip cost or deposit before ticketing
- **Escrow / trust accounts**: hold customer money separately when required by regulation
- **Payment windows**: match client payment deadlines to supplier payment terms
- **Reserve buffer**: maintain cash reserves for refunds, chargebacks, and disruption costs

## Operational best practices
- Reconcile every booking with a payment schedule and supplier settlement date
- Flag any itinerary that requires supplier payment before client funds clear
- Automate commission tracking and supplier offset calculations
- Keep audit trails for vendor invoices, client receipts, and refund authorizations

## Why this matters
Effective settlement and cashflow management protect the agency from liquidity risk and ensure travelers are not left waiting due to supplier or credit delays.
