# Creator Travel Regulatory Playbook — Latin America

This document is a market-specific playbook for creator-focused travel products in Latin America and Central America. It is written to support creator travel platforms, local agents, and partner ecosystems in markets like Guatemala, Costa Rica, Colombia, Mexico, and Peru.

## 1. Why Latin America matters
- Latin America is an important creator destination and a growing creator economy in its own right.
- Agents across the region can use the same app for local travel services, creator packages, and cross-border bookings.
- Local compliance is often fragmented: national tourism rules, regional safety expectations, informal supplier markets, and stakeholder risk.

## 2. Regulatory dimensions

### 2.1 Cross-border and local immigration
- Central America and Latin America have a mix of visa-free travel, e-visas, and on-arrival requirements.
- Agents in Guatemala may book travelers across regional corridors such as Guatemala-Mexico, Guatemala-Panama, or Guatemala-Belize.
- Visa rules for crews and paid content creators can differ from tourist/visitor rules.
- Multi-country creator itineraries need careful planning for stopovers, transit visas, and permit timelines.

### 2.2 Local tourism and supplier regulation
- Many supplier experiences are regulated by national tourism ministries, while local permits may also be required for events, tours, and filming.
- In Guatemala and similar markets, eco-tourism and community experiences often require local community approvals, cultural permissions, and responsible sourcing commitments.
- Supplier credentialing must verify guides, tour operators, and lodging against both local registration and quality expectations.

### 2.3 Consumer protection and dispute risk
- Consumer protection regimes vary widely; some markets have strong tourism laws, others leave more room for agent-level policy.
- Agent-facing use in Guatemala should account for local complaint handling, refund rights, and destination-specific dispute norms.
- Transparent terms matter because customers and creators can still pursue local consumer protection or social media escalation.

### 2.4 Fraud and financial risk
- Informal bookings, cash-based suppliers, and cross-border payment friction increase fraud risk.
- Agents in Guatemala may deal with local suppliers who lack digital payment infrastructure or formal invoicing.
- Use escrow, partner deposit controls, and evidence-based settlement workflows to reduce chargeback / fraud exposure.

### 2.5 Creator and partner compliance
- Creator content partnerships in the region require local brand-safety sensitivity, disclosure clarity, and respect for Indigenous or protected sites.
- Platform workflows should support local partner onboarding, sustainability disclosures, and cultural permitting.
- Agent-facing product flows must be flexible to handle local payment terms, deposits, and supplier risk tiers.

## 3. Product implications

### 3.1 Local agent-first product features
- Enable agents to book local supplier experiences, manage cash/split payments, and capture permit status.
- Build a local compliance layer for Latin America that includes supplier registration checks and community approval flags.
- Offer a “Guatemala creator travel bundle” or similar market-specific package type with local eco-tourism and event options.

### 3.2 Partner and supplier onboarding
- Require local partners to upload proof of registration, insurance, and special permits where applicable.
- Introduce partner risk scoring for categories such as hospitality, guided tours, event venues, and content production support.
- Support supplier onboarding for smaller local operators through templated compliance checklists.

### 3.3 Operational readiness
- Provide agents with a regional risk dashboard showing visa, permit, and complaint flags.
- Create workflows for handling disputes where the agent, creator, or customer is in a different country.
- Use local market playbooks to train agents on common complaint types and fine risk in Guatemala, Mexico, Colombia, and Peru.
