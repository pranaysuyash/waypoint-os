# Creator Travel Regulatory Playbook — United States

This document is a market-specific playbook for creator-focused travel products in the United States. It turns US regulatory risk into operational guardrails, partner requirements, content hooks, and product paths.

## 1. Why the US matters
- The US is a major creator market and global travel source, with high spending power and strong creator brand activity.
- US regulatory complexity is layered: federal travel/immigration rules, FTC influencer disclosure, state labor laws, and local permit regimes.
- For creator travel platforms, U.S. compliance is both a trust signal and a differentiation lever.

## 2. Regulatory dimensions

### 2.1 Immigration and entry rules
- ESTA / B-1/B-2 visas for short work-style travel.
- B-1/B-2 status may be appropriate for creators filming sponsored travel if the primary activity is business-related and no unauthorized paid labor occurs.
- Visa waiver program rules, length of stay, and re-entry restrictions for itineraries with frequent US visits.
- Transit and layover rules for connecting through U.S. airports, including whether a full admission is required for domestic transfers.
- I-94 admission conditions and whether creators need travel authorization for multi-leg itineraries with U.S. entry.

### 2.2 FTC influencer and advertising compliance
- Federal Trade Commission endorsement rules require clear and conspicuous disclosure of sponsored travel, gifted stays, and brand collaborations.
- Travel platforms should build disclosure workflows for creators and suppliers, including co-branded partnerships, affiliate link disclosures, and paid placement.
- Content distribution guidance must reflect FTC standards for social posts, videos, livestreams, and destination marketing.
- Policy guardrails should cover "travel product review" vs "sponsored itinerary promotion" to reduce influencer risk.

### 2.3 State and local labor / production rules
- State contractor classification and payroll rules can vary significantly across California, New York, Texas, and Florida.
- When US-based creators hire local production crews, the platform should surface local labor regulations, permit needs, and insurance requirements.
- Certain states require additional entertainment production permits for commercial filming in public spaces.
- Travel and shoot budgets should account for local permit timing, union rules, and location fees in major markets.

### 2.4 Data, privacy, and consumer protection
- California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and state privacy laws apply to U.S. traveler data, creator identifiers, and partner analytics.
- Platforms that collect creator audience data, booking behavior, or influencer performance must provide explicit consent and opt-out mechanisms.
- Consumer protection expectations include transparent pricing, cancellation rights, refund policies, and package travel disclosures.
- If the product bundles U.S. travel services, it should surface terms of service and privacy policy notices in ways that comply with state and federal guidance.

### 2.5 Insurance, liability, and risk management
- Travel insurance should cover medical, cancellation, and liability risks associated with sponsored creator trips and content production.
- Platform products should differentiate between personal travel insurance and production insurance for creators booking shoots.
- Liability clauses for suppliers and travel partners must account for creator events, influencer meetups, and live content activations.
- Emergency response and evacuation protocols are especially important for creators traveling to high-attention or high-risk destinations.

## 3. Creator travel product implications

### 3.1 Product packaging
- Offer US-specific creator packages: layover optimization, creator-friendly hotel rooms, branded event production support, and local crew sourcing.
- Build a compliance layer into itinerary builder workflows for U.S. travel, flagging visa advice, FTC disclosure reminders, and permit requirements.
- Create a supplier certification path for U.S. partners that can legally support creator marketing, brand experiences, and filming.

### 3.2 Partner onboarding
- Require U.S. suppliers to document insurance, business registration, and location permit experience.
- Segment partners into categories: accommodation, production services, event venues, local tours, and transportation for creator-supported itineraries.
- Provide partner-facing guidance on FTC disclosure, local filming permits, and creator activity classification.

### 3.3 Operational workflows
- Agent workflows should include a US compliance checklist for visa status, disclosure language, local permits, and insurance coverage.
- Build a travel advisory layer that warns creators when an itinerary crosses multiple U.S. states with different production or labor regimes.
- Offer a US creator travel risk dashboard that tracks regulatory issues, partner compliance, and event activation status.
