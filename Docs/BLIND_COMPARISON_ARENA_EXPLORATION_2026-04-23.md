## Executive Summary

The idea is good, but it should not be implemented as a generic "LMSYS for travel" gimmick.

The strong version of this idea inside Waypoint is an **internal blind adjudication system** that helps the agency:

1. compare human judgment against hidden AI judgment,
2. compare one AI variant against another without brand or prompt bias,
3. generate structured ground truth for D1 autonomy tuning, D5 override learning, and D6 evaluation,
4. train owners and operators without exposing them to model-label bias.

The weak version is a flashy side-by-side demo that produces noisy votes but does not improve operations.

The right framing is therefore:

- not a public leaderboard first,
- not a model-marketing arena,
- not a replacement for trip review,
- but a governed **evaluation lab** for decision quality, sourcing quality, suitability quality, and traveler-facing output quality.

---

## What Problem This Solves

Waypoint already has the shape of a governed system:

- D1 defines the autonomy gate between raw system judgment and effective action,
- D5 defines override learning,
- D6 defines eval progression from golden-path to shadow to gating,
- and the new settings/audit contracts define where governed behavior should live.

What it still lacks is a strong way to answer questions like:

- When the owner disagrees with the AI, who is usually right?
- Are prompt/policy changes actually improving decisions, or just sounding better?
- Is the system winning because it is better, or because evaluators know which side is "the AI"?
- Which AI worker configuration should be promoted from shadow to active?

That is exactly what a blind comparison arena can solve.

## External Reference Patterns

## 1. LMSYS Chatbot Arena

Relevant product mechanics from LMSYS:

- anonymous randomized battles,
- side-by-side responses,
- user votes before reveal,
- reveal after vote,
- pairwise win-rate aggregation via Elo / Bradley-Terry style ranking,
- explicit concern for scalable pairwise evaluation on open-ended outputs.

Why it matters here:

- anonymity reduces label bias,
- reveal-after-vote preserves honest preference data,
- pairwise comparison works better than absolute scoring for open-ended outputs.

What does **not** transfer directly:

- Waypoint is not benchmarking general chat quality,
- the evaluation volume will be much lower,
- and the product needs operational truth, not a public leaderboard.

Source:

- LMSYS blog: `https://lmsys.org/blog/2023-05-03-arena/`

## 2. Liip "Arena mode" for chatbot admin evaluation

This is closer to the actual Waypoint use case.

Useful patterns:

- compare two pre-generated runs, not only live answers,
- show one question/task at a time,
- hide labels completely,
- shuffle A/B position per evaluator using seeded randomization,
- collect simple judgments plus free-text comments,
- allow multiple evaluators,
- analyze win rate and inter-annotator agreement.

Why it matters here:

- Waypoint will often compare decisions, drafts, or proposals already generated by the system,
- seeded position randomization matters because internal operators quickly learn patterns,
- comments explain *why* a judgment was made, which is more useful than winner-only data.

Source:

- Liip article: `https://www.liip.ch/en/blog/blind-testing-your-chatbot-arena-style`

## 3. Pairwise preference over direct scoring

Research on evaluation alignment consistently points to a useful pattern:

- direct 1-5 scoring is often noisy and biased,
- pairwise preference tends to align better with human judgment,
- but pairwise systems still need transitivity checks, confidence bounds, and bias controls.

Why it matters here:

- asking an owner "is this output an 8/10?" is weaker than asking "which of these two would you send?"
- pairwise judgments are especially useful when the work product is open-ended, like itinerary drafts, sourcing options, or rationale quality.

## Core Product Framing

The feature should be framed as:

**Blind Comparison Arena = a governed pairwise evaluation layer for operational judgment.**

That means its primary artifacts are not chats. They are:

- decisions,
- review outcomes,
- sourcing recommendations,
- suitability warnings,
- traveler-facing drafts,
- and policy variants.

## The Two Primary Modes

## Mode 1 — Human vs Hidden AI

This is the most interesting mode for Waypoint.

### Flow

1. The owner or operator makes their decision first.
2. The system separately generates its own hidden decision or artifact.
3. The user commits their decision without seeing the AI outcome.
4. The system later reveals both sides and asks for blind comparison or structured post-hoc review.
5. The result becomes a judgment artifact for learning and calibration.

### Why This Is Valuable

- removes hindsight contamination from the owner's first decision,
- measures where AI is actually stronger or weaker than human judgment,
- creates clean override-learning labels,
- teaches the owner where their intuition differs from system evidence.

### Strong Use Cases

- D1 autonomy calibration: should this class of trip stay `review` or move toward `auto`?
- review quality: when owner and AI disagree on `review_required`, who wins more often?
- sourcing quality: does the AI find better options than the owner under blind comparison?
- traveler-safe writing: which draft would the owner actually send?

### Key Constraint

The owner's answer must be committed before reveal, or the data becomes contaminated.

## Mode 2 — Hidden Variant vs Hidden Variant

This is the more classic arena mode.

### Flow

1. Two system variants generate outputs for the same case.
2. Labels are hidden.
3. The evaluator compares A vs B.
4. The result is logged with comments and optional rubric scoring.
5. Aggregated results inform promotion, rollback, or policy updates.

### What Can Vary

- prompt version,
- worker policy,
- retrieval strategy,
- reasoning style,
- model family,
- autonomy policy,
- sourcing policy,
- traveler-facing phrasing.

### Why This Is Valuable

- gives a human preference layer for AI configuration changes,
- produces evidence before a variant is promoted from shadow to active,
- creates a realistic bridge between D6 eval and live operations.

### Key Constraint

This mode can easily collapse into "which answer sounds nicer" unless the rubric is tied to business and operational quality.

---

## The Important Non-Decision

This should **not** be treated as one undifferentiated arena.

There are at least three distinct evaluation objects:

1. **Decision arena** — compare judgments like `auto/review/block`, risk flags, routing, and rationale.
2. **Artifact arena** — compare generated outputs like traveler drafts, options, and summaries.
3. **Policy arena** — compare the effects of one worker/autonomy/sourcing policy variant against another.

If these are mixed together too early, the resulting signals become muddy.

## Where This Fits In Waypoint Architecture

## 1. D1 Autonomy Gradient

The [D1 autonomy architecture](file:///Users/pranay/Projects/travel_agency_agent/Docs/ARCHITECTURE_DECISION_D1_AUTONOMY_GRADIENT_2026-04-18.md) already separates:

- raw system judgment,
- from policy-enforced action,
- from final human action.

The arena is valuable because it can compare these layers explicitly.

Example:

- raw verdict: `PROCEED_TRAVELER_SAFE`
- policy action: `review`
- human choice: `block`

The arena can ask:

- was the raw verdict right?
- was the policy gate too permissive or too conservative?
- did the human improve or degrade the outcome?

That is much richer than a simple "override happened" event.

## 2. D5 Override Learning

The [D5 override design](file:///Users/pranay/Projects/travel_agency_agent/Docs/ARCHITECTURE_DECISION_D5_OVERRIDE_LEARNING_2026-04-18.md) assumes structured disagreement signals.

Arena judgments can become a higher-quality form of override evidence because they capture:

- the competing artifacts or judgments,
- the blinded winner,
- the rationale for the choice,
- and the context of the comparison.

This is stronger than a bare override click.

### Important distinction

An override says:

- "the human changed the answer"

An arena record says:

- "the human changed the answer, here were the alternatives, here is which one won under blind comparison, and here is why"

That is much better training data.

## 3. D6 Evaluation Progression

The [D6 evaluation contract](file:///Users/pranay/Projects/travel_agency_agent/Docs/D6_EVAL_CONTRACT_DESIGN_2026-04-22.md) already defines golden-path, shadow, and gating phases.

The arena can serve as the **human preference bridge** between shadow mode and promotion.

Example progression:

1. Variant runs in shadow.
2. System collects side-by-side candidate outputs.
3. Owners evaluate blind comparisons.
4. Win rate + agreement + comments determine whether the variant is safe to promote.

This is a much better promotion path than "the new prompt felt good in one demo."

## 4. Settings And Governance

The new [settings UX contract](file:///Users/pranay/Projects/travel_agency_agent/Docs/SETTINGS_ROUTE_SUBROUTE_UX_CONTRACT_2026-04-23.md) and [audit taxonomy](file:///Users/pranay/Projects/travel_agency_agent/Docs/GOVERNANCE_AUDIT_EVENT_TAXONOMY_2026-04-23.md) imply two separate homes for this feature:

- **configuration** belongs under settings,
- **execution / evaluation work** belongs in an owner or review surface.

That means the eventual product probably needs:

- a config home in `/settings/pipeline` or `/settings/ai-workforce`,
- and an execution surface in an owner/eval/review area.

It should not live only in workbench if the goal is operational governance.

## Recommended Product Model

The cleanest framing is:

### 1. Arena As An Internal Evaluation Lab

This is the primary framing.

It is used to:

- compare hidden candidates,
- generate human preference data,
- validate promotion decisions,
- calibrate autonomy and worker policies.

### 2. Arena As A Training Surface

Secondary framing.

Human-vs-hidden-AI mode can help owners and senior agents understand where the AI is actually strong or weak.

This is valuable, but should not be the primary identity of the feature.

### 3. Arena As A Leaderboard

This should be deferred.

A leaderboard is seductive, but early on it creates the wrong incentives:

- optimize for wins instead of operational correctness,
- overfit to evaluator tastes,
- turn serious governance work into a game.

## Evaluation Rubric Design

The arena only works if "better" is meaningful.

That means the evaluator should not just choose a winner. They should judge along relevant axes.

### Candidate rubric dimensions

#### For decision arena

- correctness of outcome
- safety / compliance posture
- commercial realism
- need for escalation or review
- evidence quality

#### For artifact arena

- would-send readiness
- traveler clarity
- completeness
- trustworthiness
- tone fit

#### For sourcing arena

- quality of recommendation
- margin sanity
- supplier appropriateness
- operational realism
- explainability

### Minimum judgment payload

At minimum, the evaluator should record:

1. winner: `A`, `B`, or `tie`
2. confidence: low / medium / high
3. reason comment
4. optional structured tags

The comment is not optional fluff. It is one of the highest-value parts of the system.

---

## Reveal Policy

Reveal policy is one of the most important design decisions.

### Recommendation

For valid blind data:

1. hide labels completely during evaluation,
2. require commit before reveal,
3. reveal only after winner selection,
4. mark any post-reveal edits as non-comparable.

### Why

Without this:

- evaluators anchor on the label,
- they rationalize after seeing which side was the AI,
- and the dataset becomes less trustworthy.

This is especially important in the human-vs-hidden-AI mode.

---

## Position-Bias Controls

Any serious arena needs explicit bias controls.

### Required controls

1. **Seeded randomization** of A/B position per evaluator.
2. **Two-sided replay** for high-value comparisons when feasible.
3. **Tie option** so evaluators are not forced into fake separations.
4. **Agreement tracking** across multiple evaluators where available.
5. **Comparator-family segmentation** so owner judgments and junior-agent judgments are not treated as identical gold truth.

### Why this matters

If A is often left and B often right with no swap discipline, the system will learn noise.

## How To Score Results

## Short-term recommendation: do not start with Elo as the main metric

LMSYS uses Elo because it has massive scale and many models.

Waypoint's early version will have:

- low sample counts,
- domain-specific tasks,
- heterogeneous comparison types,
- and a need for interpretable operational evidence.

That means early scoring should focus on:

- win rate,
- task-type win rate,
- evaluator confidence,
- agreement rate,
- qualitative reason clusters,
- and downstream operational outcome when available.

## Medium-term recommendation

If volume becomes large enough, use:

- Bradley-Terry or Elo-style aggregation for candidate ranking,
- bootstrapped confidence intervals,
- task-specific sub-rankings rather than one global score.

This is especially appropriate for variant-vs-variant AI comparisons.

## What To Compare

The arena should support at least these comparison subjects conceptually.

### 1. Raw decision comparison

- AI verdict vs human verdict

### 2. Autonomy outcome comparison

- one gate policy vs another gate policy

### 3. Worker configuration comparison

- quote drafter variant A vs B
- sourcing scout policy A vs B

### 4. Traveler-safe draft comparison

- one generated output vs another

### 5. Review-handling comparison

- one escalation strategy vs another

What should be avoided early:

- comparing too many variants at once,
- mixing fundamentally different task types in one pooled score,
- and treating all comparisons as equivalent.

## Major Risks

## 1. Gamification Risk

The system may optimize for winning blind comparisons rather than being operationally right.

Example:

- a draft that sounds confident may win over a more cautious but operationally correct draft.

Mitigation:

- use task-specific rubrics,
- link outcomes back to real trip results where possible,
- do not treat arena wins as the only truth.

## 2. Label Leakage Risk

The evaluator may infer which side is AI from style or formatting.

Mitigation:

- normalize presentation,
- strip obvious style tells,
- randomize order,
- and avoid system-specific formatting cues.

## 3. Low-Volume Statistics Risk

A handful of wins can create false confidence.

Mitigation:

- require minimum sample sizes,
- show confidence bands,
- avoid overusing leaderboard-style ranking early.

## 4. Human Gold Truth Risk

The owner is not always right either.

Human-vs-AI mode should not assume human correctness by default. It should capture disagreement and later compare against downstream results.

## 5. Surface Sprawl Risk

This idea can easily create one more tool without a clear home.

Mitigation:

- keep it tied to D1/D5/D6 and settings governance,
- and avoid burying it in workbench as a forever-sidecar.

## Recommended Product Decisions

- **ACCEPT**: Treat this as an internal blind evaluation lab, not a public leaderboard first.
- **ACCEPT**: Support both conceptual modes, human-vs-hidden-AI and hidden-variant-vs-hidden-variant.
- **ACCEPT+MODIFY**: Use pairwise comparison as the core interaction, but tie it to task-specific rubrics and comments.
- **ACCEPT**: Require commit-before-reveal for valid blind data.
- **ACCEPT**: Use seeded randomization and position-bias controls.
- **DEFER**: Elo or Bradley-Terry leaderboard as the main product metaphor until sample sizes justify it.
- **DEFER**: Public or customer-facing arena surface.
- **REJECT**: Framing this first as "which model wins" rather than "which operational judgment or artifact is better."

## Best Sequencing

Even though implementation is deferred, the strategic sequence is already visible.

## Phase 0 — Exploration And Contracting

- define what the arena is for,
- define comparison objects,
- define evaluation rubrics,
- define reveal and audit rules.

## Phase 1 — Human vs Hidden AI on D1 / review decisions

Why first:

- highest product value,
- strongest D5 learning signal,
- most direct governance payoff,
- easiest to explain to an owner.

## Phase 2 — Variant vs Variant for AI worker/prompt/policy comparisons

Why second:

- better once D6 eval and worker policy surfaces are more mature,
- avoids building a generic arena before the system knows what is being compared.

## Phase 3 — Multi-evaluator and promotion workflow

- add agreement metrics,
- add promotion decisions,
- connect to settings publish and audit history.
